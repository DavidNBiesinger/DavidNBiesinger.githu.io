LiPo Battery Reader (2s-6s)

Working on projects, components are not always easily available. Reasons for this could be due to many different reasons from size limitations to the price eating up the budget rapidly.

When working on a personal project recently, I needed a component to read the battery percent of a LiPo battery and send the reading to a PCB. I went to the internet in search for the component but instantly ran into some issues. Results for the part instantly were either showing as components not capable of measuring the levels I was working with, or costing over 100 dollars. With a small budget, the project being smaller and a personal side project, it was apparant that creating the device would be the best option.

For the device, after researching the types of components I would need, I settled on INA3221 boards, which read the voltages of individual inputs. I also used a JST XH Adapter, which reads lead wires from the input battery, along with an Arduino NANO, to compute the numbers and turn them into a single output for another computer to use as needed.

The project was a success but is still ongoing. With minor code issues and the need for a final assembly, the project is well on its way to becoming a an excelent product with ease of use for consumers. Going forward, the next step in the project is to optimize the electronic placement to have a tighter profile, alond with designing and manufacturing a container to make it stand out as a stand alone component.

Wall-E

The initial project this product was intended for is a replica robot I have been creating of the animated robot WALL-E. However, this is not the only project this type of device is useful for. Where I have found issues with finding devices to read the life of a battery, I have heard from peers in the college of engineering during my time at USU, that they too have encountered similar issues finding this type of device.

The purpose of this product on the replica of WALL-E is to read the battery percent, and send it to the main computer. There it is sent to a LCD screen on the front of the control panel of the robot which displays battery percentage in a manner that recreates the iconic look and function of the robot.
